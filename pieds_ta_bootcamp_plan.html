What is technical analysis?

Technical analysis (TA) studies historical price and volume to identify trends, levels, and repeatable setups. It assumes that market psychology and liquidity show up in charts before headlines fully digest. TA does not predict with certainty — it helps you trade with defined risk when probabilities favor your plan.

Five pillars of this bootcamp

  • Price action — candles and patterns
  • Structure — trend, S/R, moving averages
  • Momentum & volatility — RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands
  • Formations — H&S, flags, triangles, etc.
  • Process — multi-timeframe bias, sizing, journal

Golden rules for attendees

  • Never risk more than 1–2% of capital on one trade.
  • Always know your stop and target before entry.
  • Context beats isolated patterns.
  • Paper trade until your checklist is consistent.

Extended reference · Charts & candlesticks

Candlestick charts pack open, high, low, and close (OHLC) into one bar so you can read sentiment quickly. They originated in 18th-century Japan for rice trading and are now standard on every major platform (TradingView, NSE/BSE terminals). Patterns are probabilistic — reliability rises with trend context, support/resistance, and volume confirmation.

Core anatomy: The body spans open to close; wicks/shadows show the session high and low. A long lower wick with a small body at the top often signals rejection of lower prices (buying pressure). Color convention: green/white = close above open (bullish); red/black = close below open (bearish).

Hammer1Bullish reversal (at support)Sellers pushed down; buyers reclaimed close
Shooting star1Bearish reversal (at resistance)Buyers pushed up; sellers sold into strength

Harami2Reversal (weaker)Small inside candle — trend losing steam

Chart types compared

  • Line chart — closing prices only; good for macro trend, hides intraday detail.
  • Bar chart (OHLC) — full range per period; less visual than candles.
  • Candlestick — OHLC plus color; best for pattern recognition and teaching.
  • Heikin-Ashi — smoothed candles; trends look cleaner but lag real price.

Timeframes (what each is for)

  • 1m–15m — scalping / intraday noise; patterns expire fast.
  • 1H–4H — swing entries; combine with daily bias.
  • Daily — standard for pattern reliability (one candle = one session).
  • Weekly — macro trend and major S/R; filters lower-TF trades.

FA vs TA (quick contrast)

Fundamental analysis values a company or economy (earnings, GDP, policy). Technical analysis studies price and volume history to find repeatable behavior. Many traders use FA for what to watch and TA for when to act. Neither guarantees outcomes.

  • Wait for the candle to close before calling a pattern — mid-candle shapes often disappear.
  • Backtests suggest raw patterns alone win ~50–55% of the time; with trend + S/R + volume, edges can reach ~60–70%.
  • Three-candle patterns (morning/evening star) are generally more reliable than single-candle signals.
  • Thomas Bulkowski’s pattern statistics (thepatternsite.com) are a widely cited benchmark for pattern performance.
  • Indian context: practice on NIFTY 50, BANK NIFTY, and liquid large-caps (Reliance, TCS) for clean candles.

Extended reference · Trend, S/R & moving averages

Support and resistance are price zones where buying or selling pressure historically concentrated. Moving averages smooth price to reveal trend and often act as dynamic support/resistance. Crossovers (golden/death cross) signal longer-term sentiment shifts but work best as filters, not standalone buy signals.

Definitions: Support — floor where buyers tend to enter; price struggles to fall below. Resistance — ceiling where sellers tend to appear. Role reversal — broken support often becomes resistance on retest (and vice versa). Uptrend = higher highs + higher lows; downtrend = lower highs + lower lows.
MA typeFormula ideaBehaviorCommon use
SMAAverage of last N closesSlower, equal weight200 SMA for long-term trend filter
EMAWeighted toward recent pricesFaster reaction20/50 EMA for swing entries
Golden cross50 crosses above 200Bullish regime signalConfirm with price above both MAs
Death cross50 crosses below 200Bearish regime signalOften lagging — trend may be mature

Drawing S/R & trendlines

  • Connect at least two swing lows (support) or highs (resistance); three touches strengthen the level.
  • Zones beat exact prices — markets are messy; use areas, not pixel-perfect lines.
  • Round numbers (e.g. NIFTY 24,000) and prior day high/low often act as psychological levels.
  • Fibonacci retracements (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%) are popular short-term S/R tools on pullbacks.

Key MA periods

  • 20 — short-term trend / swing mean (about one trading month).
  • 50 — medium-term; institutional watch level.
  • 200 — long-term bull/bear divider on daily charts.
  • Price above rising 200 MA → broadly bullish environment; below → defensive bias.

Confluence example (Day 1 + 2)

Hammer at horizontal support + 50 EMA + prior swing low + RSI not overbought = multiple independent reasons to watch for a bounce. One signal alone is weak; stacked signals define A+ setups.

  • In strong uptrends, “overbought” oscillators can stay elevated — trade with the trend until structure breaks.
  • Golden crosses are bullish but often late; many traders wait for a pullback to the 50 MA instead of chasing the crossover bar.
  • Death/golden crosses can whipsaw in sideways markets — check ADX or slope of 200 MA for trend strength.
  • Dynamic S/R: in uptrends price often bounces off 20/50 EMA; in downtrends those same lines act as resistance.

Frame the problem
Ask: "You've identified an uptrend and see a hammer at support — but how do you know if price has enough momentum to follow through?" That's the job of indicators. Differentiate: leading indicators (RSI, Stochastic) attempt to predict. Lagging indicators (MACD, MAs) confirm. Neither is perfect — they complement price action.
Leading vs lagging
5–20 min
RSI — Relative Strength Index
RSI measures speed and magnitude of price moves on a 0–100 scale. Above 70 = overbought (potential reversal or pause). Below 30 = oversold (potential bounce). Midline at 50 = trend bias — above = bullish, below = bearish.

The real power: RSI divergence. Bullish divergence: price makes lower lows, RSI makes higher lows — buyers are gaining strength. Bearish divergence: price makes higher highs, RSI makes lower highs — trend weakening. Show 2 real examples of divergence leading to reversals. Warn: overbought doesn't mean "sell immediately" — strong trends can stay overbought for long.

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence
MACD = 12 EMA minus 26 EMA. Signal line = 9 EMA of MACD. Histogram = MACD minus signal line (shows momentum strength). Three signals: (1) MACD crossing above signal line = bullish momentum signal. (2) MACD crossing zero line = trend confirmation. (3) Histogram shrinking before a cross = early warning. Also show MACD divergence — similar to RSI divergence but on a histogram. Overlay both RSI and MACD on a single chart and discuss which signal to trust when they disagree.

Bollinger Bands & volume
Bollinger Bands: 20 SMA as middle band, +2/-2 standard deviations as outer bands. Price touches upper band = potentially overbought in that context. Price touches lower band = potentially oversold. Band squeeze (bands narrowing) = low volatility consolidation, predicts a big move coming. Breakout after squeeze = high-conviction directional move.

Volume: Volume confirms moves. High volume on a breakout = real. Low volume on a breakout = suspect (likely to fail). Volume spike on a reversal candle = strong signal. Show: bullish engulfing on 3x average volume vs on low volume — very different implications.

Extended reference · RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands & volume

Indicators summarize price/volume math — they describe recent behavior, not the future. RSI (Wilder, 1978) measures momentum speed. MACD (Appel, 1970s) tracks relationship between two EMAs. Bollinger Bands (Bollinger, 1980s) frame volatility. Use them as confirmation with price action, not as crystal balls.

RSI14 periods, 0–100 scale>70 overbought, <30 oversold; divergence; 50 midline biasSelling every >70 in a strong uptrend
MACD12, 26, 9 EMALine vs signal cross; zero-line cross; histogram shrink/growTrading every tiny cross in chop
Bollinger Bands20 SMA ± 2σBand touch, %B, squeeze → expansion breakoutTreating upper band touch as automatic short
Volumevs 20-day avgBreakout + high volume = conviction; low volume = suspectIgnoring volume on pattern breaks

RSI formula (concept)

RS = (average gain over N periods) ÷ (average loss over N periods). RSI = 100 − (100 ÷ (1 + RS)). RSI describes recent strength — not guaranteed reversal. In trends, use 40/60 or divergence instead of rigid 30/70.

MACD components

  • MACD line = EMA(12) − EMA(26)
  • Signal line = EMA(9) of MACD line
  • Histogram = MACD − signal (often turns before the cross)

Bollinger squeeze workflow

  1. Band width = (Upper − Lower) ÷ Middle × 100
  2. Width at multi-month low → volatility compression
  3. Wait for expansion + breakout direction
  4. Confirm with RSI/MACD and volume
Confluence stack (example): Daily uptrend + price at lower Bollinger Band + RSI bullish divergence + MACD histogram turning up + volume spike on hammer = higher-quality long setup than any single indicator alone.
  • Leading (RSI, Stochastic) — anticipate; more false signals in ranges.
  • Lagging (MACD, MAs) — confirm; late in fast reversals.
  • ~95% of price theoretically sits within ±2σ bands (normal distribution assumption — markets fat-tail).
  • On NSE, compare volume to 20-day average; delivery % adds context for institutional participation.
  • Limit yourself to 2–3 indicators you can explain without looking at notes.

Day 4 · 60 min
Chart patterns — reversal & continuation
Predicting the next move from how price has been consolidating
Difficulty: Intermediate–Advanced
0–5 min
Framing: patterns as compressed battles
Each chart pattern represents a battle between buyers and sellers played out over many candles. The resolution (breakout direction) tells you who won. Introduce the two families: reversal patterns (trend is likely ending) and continuation patterns (trend is pausing before resuming).
Reversal vs continuation
5–28 min
Reversal patterns
Head and Shoulders (H&S): Most reliable reversal. Left shoulder → Head (higher high) → Right shoulder (lower high) → Neckline break = entry. Price target = height of head measured from neckline downward. Show inverse H&S for bullish reversal.

Double Top / Double Bottom: Two touches at the same level. Break of the middle trough (double top) = bearish. Break of the middle peak (double bottom) = bullish. Price target = height of the pattern.

Rounding top / bottom: Gradual shift in momentum, less common. Show real examples of all three on index/stock charts. For each: where to enter, where to place stop-loss, how to calculate the target.
H&SDouble top/bottomEntry, SL, targetReal chart examples
28–48 min
Continuation patterns
Flags & Pennants: A sharp move (flagpole) followed by a tight consolidation against the trend. Breakout resumes the original move. Target = flagpole length projected from breakout. Bull flag vs bear flag.

Triangles: Ascending triangle (flat top, rising bottom = buyers getting aggressive → bullish breakout likely). Descending triangle (flat bottom, falling top = bearish). Symmetrical triangle (either direction, wait for breakout with volume).

Cup and handle: Rounded U-shaped base followed by a smaller pullback (handle) → breakout from handle resistance is the entry. Common in growth stocks. Discuss fake breakouts — the enemy of pattern trading — and how volume filters them out.

Extended reference · Chart patterns

Chart patterns map repeated battles between buyers and sellers. Reversal patterns suggest trend exhaustion; continuation patterns suggest pause before resumption. Always require neckline/boundary break + volume before acting. Measured-move targets are guides, not guarantees.

Head & shoulders structure

Three peaks: left shoulder, higher head, lower right shoulder. Neckline connects swing lows between shoulders. Distribution phase — buyers lose control. Confirm only on neckline break; retest of neckline as resistance can offer better R:R than chasing the first break.

Entry styles

  • Breakout entry — act immediately on confirmed close beyond boundary (faster, more false breaks).
  • Retest entry — wait for pullback to broken neckline/S/R (slower, often better R:R).

Fake breakouts

Low-volume breaks, wide spreads near news, and breaks without follow-through candles often fail. Filter with volume ≥ 1.5× average and alignment with higher-timeframe trend.

  • Never trade a pattern before the neckline or boundary closes on your timeframe.
  • H&S with a sloping neckline is valid but harder — adjust measured move accordingly.
  • Flags should slope against the prior sharp move (flagpole); pennants are small symmetrical triangles after a pole.
  • Wedges (rising/falling) can act as reversal or continuation — context and volume decide.
  • Bulkowski’s Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns ranks reliability by pattern type and market — useful for prioritizing what to teach first.

Multi-timeframe analysis (MTF)
The higher the timeframe, the more significant the level. Use the "zoom out first" approach: weekly chart for macro trend → daily chart for key SR levels and patterns → 4H or 1H for entry triggers. If the weekly trend is down but the daily shows a bull flag, it's a lower-conviction trade. Highest conviction = all three timeframes aligned. Demonstrate: open a weekly NIFTY chart, then zoom into daily, then 4H, showing how each level of detail adds information.

Building a confluence-based setup
Walk through a full real-life setup checklist: (1) What's the HTF trend? (2) Is price at a significant SR zone? (3) Is there a candlestick pattern at the level? (4) Is an MA nearby (dynamic support)? (5) What do RSI and MACD say? (6) Is volume confirming? Demonstrate this on a real historical trade (use a past breakout or reversal on NIFTY/Reliance). Setup checklistLive walkthrough
22–38 min
Risk management — the most important session
Open bluntly: "Even a 40% win rate can be profitable with good risk management." Core concepts:

Stop-loss: Non-negotiable. Below SR / below pattern / below MA. Never wider than you can afford per trade.

Risk-reward ratio (RRR): Minimum 1:2 — risk ₹1 to make ₹2. At 1:3, you can be wrong 67% of the time and still break even. Calculate RRR for every trade before entering.

Position sizing: Risk only 1–2% of total capital per trade. Formula: Position size = (Capital × Risk%) ÷ (Entry − Stop). Work through a numerical example with real numbers.

Trailing stops: Lock in profits as price moves in your favor. Move stop to breakeven once 1R is hit.

Extended reference · Strategy, MTF & risk management

Professional trading is less about prediction and more about process: define bias on a higher timeframe, enter on a lower one, risk a fixed % per trade, and target favorable reward relative to stop distance. Even ~40% win rate can be profitable if losers are small and winners are larger (positive expectancy).

WeeklyMacro biasBull, bear, or range; major S/R; ignore noise

6-point confluence checklist

  1. HTF trend direction aligned?
  2. Price at major S/R or round number?
  3. Candlestick pattern at the level?
  4. MA (20/50/200) supporting the idea?
  5. RSI/MACD confirming (no major divergence against you)?
  6. Volume confirming the move or break?

Position sizing (percent risk)

Account risk = Capital × Risk% (e.g. ₹1,00,000 × 1% = ₹1,000 max loss).
Shares = Account risk ÷ (Entry − Stop) per share.
Example: Entry ₹500, stop ₹485 (₹15 risk) → ₹1,000 ÷ 15 ≈ 66 shares.

Risk–reward math

RRR = (Target − Entry) ÷ (Entry − Stop). At 1:2, one winner covers two losers of equal size. At 1:3, break-even win rate ≈ 25%. Always compute RRR before entry — skip trades below your minimum (often 1:2).

Sample trade plan template: Instrument · HTF bias · Setup type · Entry trigger · Stop · Target · RRR · Position size · Invalidation (what proves you wrong) · Max hold time · Journal note after close.
  • Stop-loss — below pattern low, below neckline, or below nearest S/R; never “hope” without a defined exit.
  • Trailing stop — move to breakeven after +1R; trail under higher lows in trends.
  • ATR — use Average True Range to size stops in volatile names (wider stop → smaller position).
  • Paper trade 30+ setups before sizing real capital; journal every trade (screenshot + checklist score).
  • Avoid oversized positions before RBI policy, Budget, or heavy earnings — gap risk breaks technical stops.

Expectancy (why R:R matters)

Expectancy = (Win% × Avg win) − (Loss% × Avg loss). Positive expectancy over many trades is the goal — not winning every trade. Discipline beats prediction.
